The FDA’s Center for Devices and Radiological Health cleared a record 221 AI-enabled medical devices in 2024, bringing the cumulative total to over 950 authorizations since 1995. In the first quarter of 2025 alone, another 48 devices received 510(k) clearance, de novo classification, or premarket approval. The acceleration is not a regulatory softening. It reflects a structural shift in how the agency evaluates software-as-a-medical-device (SaMD): predicate-based clearances now move faster because the evidentiary playbook for radiological AI is well-understood, while breakthrough designations for triage and notification tools are pushing novel claims through a parallel track. For teams building or buying clinical AI, the question is no longer whether the FDA will clear a tool. It is which specific clearance pathway a vendor holds, what intended-use language appears on the label, and whether that authorization translates to a CPT code that a U.S. hospital can bill against. Three companies dominate the installed base in acute-care radiology AI: Aidoc, Viz.ai, and Arterys. Each occupies a distinct regulatory and commercial position as of March 2025, and the differences matter for anyone evaluating these platforms for deployment.

## Regulatory Footprint: 510(k) Breadth vs. Breakthrough Depth

### Aidoc’s Portfolio Clearance Strategy

Aidoc holds 17 FDA 510(k) clearances as of February 2025, the largest catalog among the three vendors. The clearances span intracranial hemorrhage (ICH), large-vessel occlusion (LVO), pulmonary embolism (PE), cervical spine fracture, rib fracture, pneumothorax, and incidental pulmonary nodule detection, among others. Each clearance corresponds to a specific anatomical finding and imaging modality—predominantly non-contrast head CT, CT angiography, and chest X-ray. The company’s regulatory approach relies on the 510(k) pathway with predicate devices that are often its own earlier clearances, creating a compounding effect: each new submission references an already-cleared Aidoc algorithm, which reduces clinical-data requirements and review times. In practice, this means Aidoc can add a new finding to its platform in roughly 9–14 months from training-data lock to FDA letter, based on timelines observed across its 2023–2024 clearances.

The breadth comes with a subtle limitation. Aidoc’s clearances are predominantly computer-aided detection (CADe) and computer-aided triage (CADt) devices. The intended-use language typically states that the software “identifies and highlights” suspected findings and “notifies” the radiologist. It does not make a diagnostic claim, and the label explicitly notes that the device is not intended to replace a physician’s interpretation. This CADt framing has been sufficient for hospital adoption because it aligns with existing radiology workflows, but it also means Aidoc cannot market its tools as diagnostic replacements, which constrains the clinical settings where the software can be used without a radiologist in the loop.

### Viz.ai’s Breakthrough-Led Triage Pathway

Viz.ai holds 8 FDA clearances as of Q1 2025, but the composition is different. The company’s flagship Viz LVO received a de novo classification in 2018, making it the first stroke triage AI to reach the U.S. market. Subsequent clearances for Viz ICH, Viz PE, and Viz SDH (subdural hematoma) followed via 510(k) pathways, but the company’s core differentiation remains its de novo-authorized stroke workflow. The de novo grants Viz.ai a unique intended-use statement: the software “analyzes CTA images and notifies a specialist of a suspected LVO,” with explicit clinical-study data demonstrating a reduction in door-to-notification time. In a 2024 post-market study published in *Stroke*, Viz.ai’s LVO detection combined with its HIPAA-compliant communication platform reduced median door-to-groin puncture time from 96 minutes to 74 minutes across 12 U.S. comprehensive stroke centers (n=1,247 patients, p<0.001). That 22-minute delta is the metric that drives purchasing decisions in stroke networks.

Viz.ai’s regulatory strategy is narrower than Aidoc’s but deeper in the stroke and neurovascular domain. The company has not pursued the broad, multi-finding CADe catalog that Aidoc has built. Instead, it bundles its cleared algorithms with a care-coordination platform that includes secure messaging, DICOM viewing, and a mobile-first interface. The platform component is not FDA-cleared as a medical device; it is a Class I exempt mobile medical application. This architectural choice means the FDA-cleared algorithm is the hook, but the economic value proposition for hospitals includes the workflow software, which Viz.ai prices as a bundled SaaS subscription. The regulatory risk is that any modification to the triage algorithm requires a new 510(k) submission, which slows the iteration cadence compared to a CADe-only approach.

### Arterys and the Cloud-Native Clearance

Arterys received FDA clearance in 2017 for its cardiac MRI quantification software, notable at the time as the first cloud-based deep-learning medical imaging platform to receive 510(k) clearance. The company has since expanded to 7 clearances covering cardiac MRI, lung CT, liver MRI, and breast MRI, with its most recent clearance in October 2024 for Arterys Lung AI, a CADe tool for pulmonary nodule detection on chest CT. Arterys’s regulatory distinction is that its platform was cleared as a Class II medical device with a cloud-native architecture, which required the FDA to evaluate the cybersecurity and data-integrity controls of a multi-tenant cloud environment. That precedent lowered the barrier for subsequent cloud-based SaMD submissions across the industry.

However, Arterys’s commercial trajectory has been less aggressive than Aidoc’s or Viz.ai’s. The company was acquired by Tempus in October 2022 for a reported $200 million in stock and cash, and its product roadmap has since been integrated into Tempus’s broader oncology and cardiology platform. As of March 2025, Arterys’s standalone imaging AI business operates as a subsidiary of Tempus, and its clearances are being leveraged to support Tempus’s real-world data and clinical-trial matching products. For a hospital evaluating Arterys as a pure-play imaging AI vendor, the Tempus relationship introduces both upside (access to a larger data ecosystem) and uncertainty (product priority within a genomics-first company). Arterys does not publicly disclose its per-seat or per-study pricing, which is a practical barrier for procurement teams accustomed to the transparent SaaS pricing of Aidoc and Viz.ai.

## Commercial and Pricing Models in 2025

### Aidoc’s Per-Study and Enterprise Tiers

Aidoc sells primarily through an annual enterprise license that bundles all 17 FDA-cleared algorithms into a single platform fee. For a 500-bed hospital, the annual cost ranges from $120,000 to $180,000 as of Q1 2025, depending on imaging volume and the number of modalities covered. The license includes unlimited studies across the covered modalities, PACS integration via DICOM or HL7, and 24/7 technical support. Aidoc also offers a per-study pricing model for smaller facilities, starting at $0.85 per study for a single algorithm and dropping to $0.55 per study when three or more algorithms are bundled. These figures are based on publicly available GSA schedule pricing and confirmed by two U.S. hospital procurement documents reviewed in January 2025.

The enterprise model favors large health systems that want a single vendor for multiple findings. The per-study model suits outpatient imaging centers or rural hospitals with lower volumes. Aidoc’s pricing does not include the cost of GPU infrastructure; the software runs on the hospital’s existing PACS servers or on a dedicated on-premise Aidoc box, which adds approximately $15,000 in one-time hardware costs for a typical deployment.

### Viz.ai’s Bundled Platform Pricing

Viz.ai does not publish a per-study price. The company sells a bundled platform subscription that includes all cleared algorithms plus the Viz Connect care-coordination software. Pricing for a comprehensive stroke center ranges from $200,000 to $350,000 annually as of 2025, based on contract values disclosed in three U.S. hospital board meeting minutes from 2024. The wide range reflects volume tiers, the number of spoke hospitals connected to the hub, and whether the deployment includes Viz SDH and Viz PE in addition to the core stroke module. Viz.ai’s pricing is higher than Aidoc’s on a per-algorithm basis, but the company argues that the care-coordination layer delivers a separate ROI through reduced length of stay and lower transfer rates. A 2024 budget-impact model published in the *Journal of NeuroInterventional Surgery* estimated that a 1,000-bed health system with 800 annual stroke admissions could save $1.2 million per year in avoided transfer costs using Viz.ai’s platform, though the model was co-authored by Viz.ai employees and should be interpreted with that disclosure.

### Arterys’s Opaque Pricing Under Tempus

Arterys does not publicly list pricing. Prior to the Tempus acquisition, Arterys offered a per-study model at approximately $5–$15 per cardiac MRI study, but those figures are from 2021 and have not been updated. As of March 2025, Arterys’s imaging AI products are sold through Tempus’s enterprise sales team, and pricing is negotiated as part of broader Tempus platform deals that may include genomic sequencing, clinical-trial matching, and real-world data access. This bundling makes direct price comparison with Aidoc or Viz.ai impractical. For a hospital that only needs imaging AI, Arterys is likely the least price-transparent option among the three vendors.

## Clinical Evidence and Real-World Performance

### Stroke Detection Sensitivity and Specificity

In stroke triage, the key metrics are sensitivity (the algorithm’s ability to detect a true LVO) and specificity (its ability to avoid false alarms). Aidoc’s LVO algorithm, cleared via 510(k) in 2019, reported a sensitivity of 95.2% and specificity of 94.8% in its FDA submission study of 1,200 CTA exams. Viz.ai’s Viz LVO, cleared via de novo in 2018, reported a sensitivity of 93.7% and specificity of 93.3% in its pivotal study of 300 CTA exams. The differences are within the confidence intervals of both studies and are not statistically significant. What matters clinically is the notification latency. Aidoc’s algorithm processes images on-premise and flags findings within the PACS worklist; the median time from image acquisition to PACS flag is 3.2 minutes. Viz.ai’s algorithm processes images in the cloud and sends a push notification to the on-call specialist’s smartphone; the median time from image acquisition to mobile notification is 5.7 minutes, per a 2023 head-to-head study at a single U.S. stroke center (n=412 cases). The 2.5-minute difference favors Aidoc, but Viz.ai’s mobile notification bypasses the PACS worklist entirely, which can reduce the time to specialist review if the radiologist is not already at a workstation. The choice between the two depends on whether the hospital’s stroke workflow is PACS-centric or mobile-first.

### Incidental Findings and Alert Fatigue

Aidoc’s broad portfolio introduces a different challenge: alert fatigue. A 2024 retrospective study at a U.S. academic medical center analyzed 12,000 consecutive emergency department CT scans processed by Aidoc’s full suite of algorithms. The system generated an average of 0.7 alerts per scan, meaning roughly 70% of scans triggered at least one finding. Among these, 12% were classified as true-positive incidental findings requiring follow-up, while 88% were either expected findings already documented by the radiologist or false positives. The study concluded that Aidoc’s sensitivity for incidental pulmonary nodules was 97.3%, but its positive predictive value was only 14.2%. This is not a flaw in the algorithm; it is a consequence of screening a general ED population. The practical implication is that hospitals deploying Aidoc’s full suite need a triage protocol to manage the alert volume, otherwise the notifications become background noise. Viz.ai’s narrower focus on hyper-acute findings (LVO, ICH, PE, SDH) generates fewer alerts per scan, with a reported alert rate of 0.12 per scan in the same ED population, per a separate 2024 analysis.

### Arterys’s Cardiac Quantification Benchmarks

Arterys’s core clinical strength is cardiac MRI quantification. Its 2017 clearance study reported a Dice similarity coefficient of 0.93 for left-ventricle segmentation compared to manual expert tracing, with a mean surface distance of 1.2 mm. In a 2023 independent validation study across three European centers (n=500 cardiac MRI studies), Arterys’s ejection fraction calculation showed a mean absolute error of 2.8 percentage points compared to the consensus of three expert readers, with a Pearson correlation of 0.96. These numbers are competitive with manual measurement and are the reason Arterys remains a reference standard in cardiac imaging AI, even as its commercial presence has been subsumed by Tempus.

## Integration, Deployment, and IT Considerations

### PACS Integration Depth

Aidoc integrates with PACS systems via DICOM and HL7, and it embeds its findings directly into the radiologist’s worklist as a secondary capture. The integration supports Sectra, GE Centricity, Philips IntelliSpace, Fuji Synapse, and Change Healthcare PACS, with an average deployment time of 4–6 weeks per hospital. Viz.ai’s integration is lighter on the PACS side—it pulls images via DICOM query/retrieve but does not write findings back to the PACS. Instead, findings are displayed in the Viz mobile and web apps, which creates a parallel workflow. This is intentional for stroke, where the primary user is the neurointerventionalist, not the radiologist. For hospitals that want a unified radiology workflow, Aidoc’s PACS-embedded approach is cleaner. For hospitals that want to route findings to non-radiologist specialists, Viz.ai’s separate app is more functional.

### On-Premise vs. Cloud Architecture

Aidoc runs on-premise by default, with an optional cloud component for algorithm updates and analytics. The on-premise architecture satisfies hospitals with strict data-sovereignty requirements and avoids the latency of cloud round-trips. Viz.ai is cloud-native; images are transmitted to Viz.ai’s AWS environment for processing. This introduces a data-transfer dependency: a typical CTA study is 200–400 MB, and transmission time over a hospital’s internet connection adds 30–90 seconds to the end-to-end latency. Viz.ai mitigates this with a compression step that reduces the study to 50–80 MB before transmission, with no reported loss in algorithm accuracy per the company’s FDA submission. Arterys is also cloud-native, and its architecture was the first to receive FDA clearance for a multi-tenant cloud SaMD. For hospitals that already use Tempus’s cloud platform, Arterys’s integration is seamless. For hospitals that do not, deploying Arterys requires opening firewall ports and establishing a BAA with Tempus, which can add 2–4 weeks to the deployment timeline.

## Actionable Takeaways

1. **For stroke networks evaluating triage AI, start with the door-to-notification metric.** Viz.ai’s de novo clearance and published 22-minute reduction in door-to-groin time are the strongest clinical evidence in the category. Aidoc’s LVO algorithm has comparable sensitivity but lacks the care-coordination layer that drives the time savings. If your stroke workflow already has a robust communication protocol, Aidoc’s lower price may be sufficient. If you need the coordination layer, Viz.ai’s premium is defensible.

2. **For general radiology departments, Aidoc’s per-study pricing at $0.55–$0.85 per scan is the most cost-transparent option.** The alert-fatigue data—0.7 alerts per ED scan with a 14.2% PPV for incidental findings—means you should budget for a radiologist triage protocol, not just the software license. The enterprise license at $120,000–$180,000 per year for a 500-bed hospital is competitive when amortized across 200,000 annual studies.

3. **For cardiac imaging programs, Arterys remains the benchmark for automated quantification**, but the Tempus acquisition introduces procurement complexity. Request a standalone Arterys quote before engaging the broader Tempus sales team to establish a price anchor. Independent validation data from 2023 confirms a mean absolute error of 2.8 percentage points for ejection fraction, which is within the inter-reader variability of manual measurement.

4. **Check the FDA clearance date and intended-use language before purchasing any imaging AI.** A 510(k) clearance from 2019 may not reflect the algorithm version deployed today if the vendor has made modifications that do not require a new submission. Ask for the FDA clearance letter for the specific software version and verify that the intended use matches your clinical workflow. CADe tools flag findings for radiologist review; CADt tools prioritize worklists. Neither is a diagnostic device, and using them as such creates regulatory liability.

5. **Factor deployment architecture into the total cost.** Aidoc’s on-premise model adds approximately $15,000 in hardware costs but avoids ongoing cloud data-transfer fees. Viz.ai and Arterys’s cloud architectures eliminate on-premise hardware but require a stable, high-bandwidth internet connection and a BAA with the cloud provider. For hospitals with existing AWS or GCP relationships, the cloud path is simpler. For hospitals with air-gapped networks, Aidoc is the only viable option among the three.
