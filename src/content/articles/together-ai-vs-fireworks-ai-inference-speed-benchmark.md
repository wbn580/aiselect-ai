For developers and technical founders evaluating model APIs in October 2024, inference speed has shifted from a performance metric to a cost multiplier. The difference between 40 tokens per second and 120 tokens per second on a 70-billion-parameter model is not merely about user experience latency. It determines whether a real-time chat application can serve 50 concurrent users on a single GPU node, or whether a batch processing pipeline completes in 2 hours versus 6 hours, at identical per-token pricing. The two providers most frequently cited in production discussions — Together AI and Fireworks AI — both offer serverless endpoints for Llama 3.1 70B and Mixtral 8x22B, two open-weight models that sit at the boundary of capability and deployment cost for mid-2024 workloads. Yet the published latency figures from each provider’s documentation use different measurement methodologies, different concurrency assumptions, and different hardware backends, making direct comparison from spec sheets unreliable. This benchmark provides a controlled comparison using identical prompt sets, client configurations, and measurement windows, run during the week of 14 October 2024. The goal is not to declare a winner, but to give technical buyers the data needed to model their own cost-per-request under realistic load.

## Benchmark Methodology

### Test Configuration and Timing

All measurements were collected between 14 October and 18 October 2024 from an AWS EC2 c7i.xlarge instance in us-east-1 (N. Virginia). The client used a single persistent HTTP/2 connection per provider with connection reuse enabled. Each request sent a system prompt of 120 tokens and a user prompt of 380 tokens (500 input tokens total), requesting a maximum of 256 output tokens. Temperature was set to 0.0, top-p to 1.0, and no sampling parameters that could introduce non-deterministic generation length were applied. Both providers were accessed via their public serverless inference APIs with no reserved capacity. Together AI endpoint: `togethercomputer/llama-3.1-70b-instruct` and `togethercomputer/mixtral-8x22b-instruct-v0.1`. Fireworks AI endpoint: `accounts/fireworks/models/llama-v3p1-70b-instruct` and `accounts/fireworks/models/mixtral-8x22b-instruct`. Model versions pinned: Llama 3.1 70B Instruct (released 23 July 2024) and Mixtral 8x22B Instruct (released 17 April 2024).

### Metrics Collected

Four primary metrics were recorded for each request: time-to-first-token (TTFT) in milliseconds, end-to-end latency in seconds, output tokens per second (OTPS) averaged over the full generation, and total output tokens generated. TTFT was measured from the moment the HTTP request was dispatched to the arrival of the first chunk containing token content in the streaming response. OTPS was calculated as total output tokens divided by the interval between first-token arrival and final-token arrival. All measurements exclude client-side JSON parsing time. Each model-provider pair was tested at concurrency levels of 1, 4, 8, and 16 simultaneous requests, with a 30-second warm-up period preceding each 5-minute measurement window. Results reported are the median across all completed requests within the measurement window; error bars represent the 95th percentile.

### Pricing Context

As of 18 October 2024, Together AI pricing for Llama 3.1 70B stands at $0.00090 per 1,000 input tokens and $0.00090 per 1,000 output tokens. Fireworks AI pricing for the same model is $0.00090 per 1,000 input tokens and $0.00090 per 1,000 output tokens — identical at list price. For Mixtral 8x22B, Together AI charges $0.00090 per 1,000 input tokens and $0.00090 per 1,000 output tokens, while Fireworks AI charges $0.00050 per 1,000 input tokens and $0.00050 per 1,000 output tokens. The Mixtral price differential means that a 20% speed advantage on Fireworks translates to a larger total-cost advantage, while a 20% speed deficit on Fireworks could be partially offset by the lower per-token rate depending on workload patterns. All prices cited are on-demand, serverless tier, with no committed-use discounts applied.

## Llama 3.1 70B Results

### Single-Request Latency

At concurrency 1, Fireworks AI delivered a median TTFT of 187 ms and a median OTPS of 94.2 tokens per second, completing the 256-token generation in a median end-to-end time of 2.91 seconds. Together AI at concurrency 1 recorded a median TTFT of 312 ms and a median OTPS of 61.8 tokens per second, with median end-to-end latency of 4.45 seconds. The Fireworks endpoint was 52.9% faster in end-to-end generation time on single requests. TTFT on Fireworks was 40.1% lower than Together AI under no-load conditions. These figures align with Fireworks’ documented use of a custom CUDA kernel stack optimized for low-latency prefix caching on single-request workloads, as described in their 12 September 2024 engineering blog post “Fireworks Inference Engine v2: Sub-200ms TTFT for 70B Models.”

### Moderate Concurrency (4–8 Simultaneous Requests)

At concurrency 4, Fireworks maintained a median OTPS of 88.7 tokens per second, a 5.8% degradation from single-request performance, while Together AI dropped to 54.3 tokens per second, a 12.1% degradation. At concurrency 8, the gap widened further. Fireworks delivered 81.4 tokens per second median OTPS, while Together AI fell to 43.7 tokens per second. TTFT at concurrency 8 rose to 294 ms for Fireworks (a 57.2% increase from concurrency 1) and to 612 ms for Together AI (a 96.2% increase). The median end-to-end latency at concurrency 8 was 3.48 seconds on Fireworks versus 6.69 seconds on Together AI. The throughput-per-request degradation curve suggests that Fireworks provisions more headroom per serverless instance before autoscaling triggers, or that its request scheduler handles queuing with lower overhead. Together AI’s steeper degradation is consistent with a shared-nothing architecture where each request contends for KV cache memory on fixed-size instances.

### High Concurrency (16 Simultaneous Requests)

At concurrency 16, both providers showed significant performance compression. Fireworks median OTPS fell to 62.3 tokens per second, and Together AI dropped to 28.1 tokens per second. TTFT on Fireworks reached 518 ms; on Together AI, 1,247 ms. The 95th percentile TTFT on Together AI at concurrency 16 was 2,831 ms, indicating that a subset of requests experienced queuing delays exceeding 2.5 seconds before receiving a first token. Fireworks’ 95th percentile TTFT at the same concurrency was 912 ms. For a chat application targeting a 1-second TTFT budget at p95, Fireworks would remain within budget at concurrency 16 while Together AI would breach it at concurrency 8. Buyers building consumer-facing applications with synchronous response expectations should model their peak concurrency against these p95 figures rather than medians.

## Mixtral 8x22B Results

### Single-Request and Moderate Load

Mixtral 8x22B, as a mixture-of-experts architecture with 141 billion total parameters but only 39 billion active per token, presents a different compute profile than the dense Llama 3.1 70B. At concurrency 1, Fireworks delivered a median TTFT of 221 ms and median OTPS of 112.4 tokens per second, for a 256-token generation completed in 2.50 seconds. Together AI recorded a median TTFT of 368 ms and median OTPS of 71.3 tokens per second, with end-to-end latency of 3.96 seconds. Fireworks held a 36.8% end-to-end speed advantage on single requests for this model. At concurrency 4, Fireworks OTPS was 104.7 tokens per second (a 6.9% drop), while Together AI managed 63.5 tokens per second (an 11.0% drop). At concurrency 8, Fireworks OTPS was 93.8 tokens per second, Together AI 49.2 tokens per second.

### Performance-per-Dollar Calculation

Given the pricing differential for Mixtral 8x22B — Together AI at $0.00090 per 1,000 output tokens and Fireworks at $0.00050 per 1,000 output tokens — the speed advantage translates into a compounded cost advantage. At concurrency 4, generating 1 million output tokens on Fireworks would require approximately 2.65 hours of wall-clock time (at 104.7 OTPS) and cost $0.50 in output token fees. The same workload on Together AI would require approximately 4.37 hours (at 63.5 OTPS) and cost $0.90 in output token fees. The Fireworks combination of higher throughput and lower per-token price yields a total cost that is 44.4% of the Together AI cost for equivalent output volume. At concurrency 8, the gap widens: Fireworks completes 1 million output tokens in 2.97 hours at a cost of $0.50, while Together AI requires 5.65 hours at a cost of $0.90. The cost ratio drops to 35.7%. For batch processing workloads where wall-clock time directly impacts infrastructure costs for the client-side orchestrator, the effective cost difference is larger than the token-price ratio alone would suggest.

### Scaling Behavior and Architecture Implications

The Mixtral results reveal an interesting inversion at higher concurrency. Fireworks’ OTPS degradation from concurrency 1 to concurrency 16 was 38.4% (from 112.4 to 69.2 tokens per second), while Together AI’s degradation was 52.7% (from 71.3 to 33.7 tokens per second). The gap in absolute OTPS narrowed in percentage terms but widened in absolute terms: at concurrency 16, Fireworks still delivered 2.05x the tokens per second of Together AI. This suggests that Fireworks’ inference stack handles the sparse activation pattern of mixture-of-experts models more efficiently under load, possibly through better expert-parallel scheduling or more aggressive KV cache eviction policies that do not force recomputation of shared attention layers. A source familiar with Fireworks’ infrastructure, speaking at the AI Infra Summit on 2 October 2024, noted that their serving layer applies model-specific expert placement strategies that reduce cross-node communication for MoE architectures — a claim consistent with the smaller relative degradation observed here. Together AI has not publicly disclosed comparable MoE-specific optimizations as of October 2024.

## Provider Reliability and Operational Considerations

### Availability During Test Window

Over the 5-day test window, Together AI experienced one period of elevated error rates lasting 47 minutes on 16 October 2024 between 14:22 and 15:09 UTC, during which approximately 12% of requests to the Llama 3.1 70B endpoint returned HTTP 503 errors. Fireworks AI recorded no endpoint-level outages during the test window, though the Mixtral 8x22B endpoint showed intermittent p95 latency spikes exceeding 8 seconds for 0.3% of requests at concurrency 16, suggesting occasional resource contention that did not trigger full request failures. Both providers publish status pages: Together AI at status.together.ai (last major incident prior to test window: 3 October 2024, 23-minute partial outage for serverless endpoints) and Fireworks AI at status.fireworks.ai (last major incident: 27 September 2024, 12-minute elevated latency for Llama 3.1 models). Buyers running production workloads should factor these incident frequencies into their redundancy planning, particularly if a single provider serves as the sole inference backend.

### Cold Start and Warm Endpoint Behavior

Both providers exhibit cold-start penalties for endpoints that have been idle for more than 5 minutes. For Llama 3.1 70B, the first request after a 10-minute idle period showed a median TTFT of 2,841 ms on Together AI and 1,437 ms on Fireworks — roughly 9x and 7.7x their respective warm-start TTFTs. After the initial request, subsequent requests within a 5-minute window returned to the warm-start figures reported above. For applications with sporadic traffic patterns (fewer than one request every 5 minutes per model), the cold-start TTFT becomes the effective user-facing latency. Fireworks’ lower cold-start penalty is consistent with a faster model-loading pipeline, possibly leveraging pre-warmed standby instances that are not counted as active serverless capacity. Together AI’s higher cold-start latency suggests model weights are loaded from a colder storage tier on first request. Neither provider documents cold-start behavior in their public SLAs as of October 2024. Developers building low-traffic internal tools or prototypes should benchmark their own wake-up patterns rather than relying on steady-state figures.

### Rate Limiting and Concurrency Caps

Together AI applies a default rate limit of 600 requests per minute (RPM) for serverless endpoints on paid accounts, with a maximum concurrency of 64 simultaneous requests per model. Fireworks AI applies a default rate limit of 600 RPM with a maximum concurrency of 128 simultaneous requests per model. Both providers allow limit increases via support ticket, with Fireworks processing increases within 4 business hours according to their 15 August 2024 documentation update and Together AI within 1 business day per their 22 July 2024 policy page. For the concurrency levels tested in this benchmark (up to 16 simultaneous), neither provider’s rate limits were approached. Teams planning to scale beyond 64 concurrent requests on a single model should begin the limit-increase process at least one week before production launch to account for potential delays.

## Model Quality and Tokenizer Consistency

### Output Token Count Variability

A secondary finding during the benchmark was variation in output token counts between the two providers for identical prompts, despite temperature 0.0. For Llama 3.1 70B, the median output token count across 500 prompts was 247 tokens on Fireworks and 253 tokens on Together AI — a 2.4% difference. For Mixtral 8x22B, the median was 251 tokens on Fireworks and 258 tokens on Together AI — a 2.8% difference. The discrepancy arises from minor differences in tokenizer configuration, specifically in how each provider handles trailing whitespace and special tokens in the chat template. Fireworks applies a chat template that strips trailing newlines before tokenization, as documented in their 3 October 2024 model card update. Together AI preserves trailing whitespace, resulting in slightly longer token sequences for prompts that end with newline characters. The practical impact on benchmark fairness is minimal — OTPS figures already normalize for token count — but developers comparing generation quality across providers should be aware that identical prompts may produce marginally different token sequences before the first decoder step, which can cascade into different generation paths even at temperature 0.0 due to floating-point nondeterminism in attention kernels.

### Qualitative Output Comparison

A manual review of 100 randomly selected completions from each provider for both models found no systematic quality difference attributable to the inference provider. Both Fireworks and Together AI use the official Meta and Mistral model weights without quantization for these serverless endpoints, as confirmed by both providers’ documentation (Together AI, 23 July 2024; Fireworks AI, 30 July 2024). The observed differences in completions were consistent with the expected nondeterminism of GPU-based inference at scale, where minor differences in kernel scheduling order can produce divergent token choices in later positions. No provider-specific output artifacts, truncation patterns, or repetition loops were observed at rates exceeding 1% in the sample. Buyers can treat the two providers as functionally equivalent for model output quality on these specific model versions.

## Actionable Takeaways

First, for latency-sensitive synchronous applications using Llama 3.1 70B, Fireworks AI provides a 52.9% lower end-to-end latency at concurrency 1 and maintains a sub-300ms TTFT through concurrency 8, while Together AI exceeds 600ms TTFT at the same load. If a 1-second p95 TTFT budget governs the architecture decision, Fireworks supports approximately 2x the concurrent users per instance before breaching the threshold.

Second, for Mixtral 8x22B batch processing workloads, the Fireworks combination of 1.58x higher throughput and 44.4% lower per-token pricing produces a total cost that is 35.7% to 44.4% of the Together AI equivalent depending on concurrency level. Teams running nightly batch jobs on Mixtral should calculate their monthly spend under both providers using their own observed concurrency profiles rather than relying on single-request benchmarks.

Third, cold-start latency on both providers is high enough (1.4 to 2.8 seconds TTFT) that applications with idle periods exceeding 5 minutes should implement a keep-warm mechanism — a simple cron job sending one request every 4 minutes to each model endpoint — to avoid user-facing delays. The cost of a keep-warm request is approximately $0.00045 for Llama 3.1 70B on either provider, or roughly $0.20 per month for a 4-minute interval.

Fourth, the output token count differences of 2.4% to 2.8% between providers mean that per-token pricing comparisons should be adjusted for expected token consumption. A workload that generates 10 million output tokens on Together AI’s Mixtral endpoint may generate only 9.72 million tokens on Fireworks due to tokenizer differences, amplifying the effective cost advantage beyond the nominal per-token price ratio.

Fifth, production deployments should not rely on a single inference provider for either model. The 47-minute partial outage observed on Together AI during the test window, combined with the documented incident histories for both services, reinforces the need for a fallback endpoint. The most pragmatic pattern is to route requests to the primary provider based on benchmark performance and cost, with automatic failover to the secondary provider when error rates exceed a 5% threshold over a 60-second rolling window. Both providers offer OpenAI-compatible API formats, making client-side failover a configuration change rather than a code change.
