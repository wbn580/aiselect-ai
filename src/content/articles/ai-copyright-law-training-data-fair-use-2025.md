The question of who owns the data used to train large language models has moved from academic debate to boardroom calculus. In the first quarter of 2025, three separate U.S. federal district courts issued conflicting rulings on whether ingesting copyrighted text for model training constitutes fair use. The U.S. Copyright Office released its long-awaited Part 3 guidance on artificial intelligence and copyrightability on January 29, 2025, explicitly declining to extend safe harbor provisions to unlicensed training data. Meanwhile, the European Union’s AI Act entered its second enforcement phase on February 2, 2025, requiring transparency disclosures for all general-purpose AI models trained on copyrighted material. For technical buyers evaluating model APIs and foundation models, the legal status of a model’s training corpus is no longer a footnote. It determines contractual indemnity scope, downstream liability exposure, and whether a model can be deployed in regulated sectors at all. The era of treating training data provenance as someone else’s problem ended when the first enterprise customer demanded a full data lineage audit before signing a $150,000 annual license.

## The Current Legal Landscape

### U.S. Fair Use Doctrine Under Pressure

The four-factor fair use test under 17 U.S.C. § 107 has become the primary battleground for AI training data disputes. In *New York Times Co. v. Microsoft Corp.*, the Southern District of New York ruled on February 14, 2025, that verbatim memorization of NYT articles in GPT-4’s training data did not qualify as transformative use when the model could reproduce near-exact passages with minimal prompting. The court applied factor one (purpose and character of use) and factor four (effect on the market) to deny summary judgment to OpenAI and Microsoft, explicitly noting that the existence of a licensing market for training data—publishers had offered licenses at rates averaging $0.002 per token as of Q4 2024—weighed against fair use. The case is now proceeding to trial in Q3 2025 with potential statutory damages exposure of $150,000 per infringed work.

In contrast, the Ninth Circuit’s December 2024 decision in *Kadrey v. Meta Platforms Inc.* held that training LLaMA models on books from the Books3 dataset was transformative because the model learned statistical patterns rather than storing and reproducing protected expression. The court emphasized factor two (nature of the copyrighted work), noting that factual and non-fiction works receive thinner copyright protection than creative works. This circuit split means the legality of a model’s training data currently depends on geography and the specific factual record of each case. The Supreme Court has not yet granted certiorari, leaving enterprises to navigate a patchwork where a model legally trained under Ninth Circuit precedent may still face liability in the Second Circuit.

### EU AI Act Transparency Obligations

Article 53 of the EU AI Act, effective February 2, 2025, requires providers of general-purpose AI models to publish a “sufficiently detailed summary” of the content used for training. The European AI Office issued implementing guidance on January 15, 2025, specifying that summaries must include: the categories of data sources (e.g., web crawl, licensed databases, user-generated content), the approximate volume of data per category measured in tokens, and whether copyrighted works were included and under what legal basis. Models released after the effective date without compliant summaries face fines of up to €15 million or 3% of global annual turnover, whichever is higher. For models already on the market, a grace period extends to August 2, 2025.

This transparency regime creates a practical disclosure burden for model providers. OpenAI published its first Article 53 summary for GPT-4o on January 28, 2025, acknowledging that its training data includes copyrighted material used under a fair use claim. Anthropic followed on February 10, 2025, for Claude 3.5 Sonnet, disclosing that approximately 12% of its training tokens came from licensed publisher agreements negotiated between June 2023 and December 2024. Google DeepMind’s Gemini 1.5 Pro summary, published February 18, 2025, cited both fair use and contractual licenses with “over 1,500 content partners” without specifying the proportion of each. For buyers, these summaries are now the primary due diligence documents for assessing legal risk exposure.

### Jurisdictional Fragmentation

Beyond the U.S. and EU, regulatory approaches diverge sharply. Japan’s Agency for Cultural Affairs reaffirmed in its June 2024 policy statement that ingesting copyrighted works for machine learning purposes is permitted under Article 30-4 of the Copyright Act, regardless of the purpose, provided the extraction is not for “enjoyment of the work itself.” This makes Japan the most permissive major jurisdiction for training data acquisition. The United Kingdom’s proposed AI copyright code, abandoned in February 2024 after negotiations between rights holders and AI developers collapsed, left the legal status of training data ingestion unresolved. The UK Intellectual Property Office stated on March 3, 2025, that it would introduce legislation by Q3 2025, with early signals pointing toward an opt-out system similar to the EU’s text and data mining exception under Article 4 of the DSM Directive. Singapore’s Copyright Act 2021, amended effective November 2024, introduced a computational data analysis exception in Section 244 that permits copying for data analysis purposes, including AI training, provided the user has lawful access to the material. This exception does not apply if the copyright owner has expressly reserved their rights through machine-readable means.

## Practical Implications for Model Selection

### Indemnification and Contractual Protections

Enterprise procurement teams now treat intellectual property indemnification as a threshold requirement for model API contracts. As of March 2025, the market has segmented into three tiers of protection. Tier 1 providers—OpenAI (for ChatGPT Enterprise and API customers), Microsoft (for Azure OpenAI Service), Google (for Vertex AI), and Adobe (for Firefly)—offer uncapped or high-cap indemnification for copyright infringement claims arising from training data, subject to specific usage conditions. OpenAI’s Copyright Shield, announced November 6, 2023, and expanded January 2024, covers customers who use the company’s embedded mitigations and do not deliberately prompt models to reproduce copyrighted material. Microsoft’s Customer Copyright Commitment, effective September 2023, provides similar coverage for Azure OpenAI Service customers who implement required content filters and monitoring.

Tier 2 providers—Anthropic, Cohere, and Mistral—offer limited indemnification with caps ranging from $5 million to the total fees paid over the preceding 12 months. Anthropic’s commercial terms, updated January 2025, cap liability at 12 months of fees and exclude claims arising from user prompts that intentionally elicit copyrighted content. Tier 3 providers, including most open-weight model platforms (Hugging Face, Replicate, Together AI), offer no training data indemnification. Buyers deploying models from Tier 3 providers assume full liability for any infringement claims, a risk that compounds when fine-tuning on proprietary data that may itself contain copyrighted material.

### Data Provenance and Audit Trails

The EU AI Act’s transparency requirements are forcing model providers to maintain auditable data provenance records. For buyers, the practical question is whether a provider can produce a data lineage report that satisfies enterprise compliance requirements. Databricks’ MosaicML team published a data provenance framework in December 2024 that tracks training data through five stages: acquisition source, license verification, deduplication, filtering, and tokenization. Models trained using this framework, including DBRX released March 2024, carry a provenance attestation that some enterprise legal teams now require as a condition of deployment.

Hugging Face introduced Data Cards 2.0 in November 2024, a structured metadata schema that documents dataset composition, collection methodology, licensing status, and known copyright concerns. As of March 2025, 847 datasets on the Hugging Face Hub include Data Card 2.0 documentation. For technical buyers evaluating fine-tuning datasets or pre-trained models, the presence or absence of a Data Card 2.0 provides a quick signal of data governance maturity. The absence of documentation should be treated as a red flag in any production deployment context.

### Open-Weight vs. Proprietary Models

The legal uncertainty surrounding training data has shifted the risk calculus for open-weight models. Models like Meta’s Llama 3.1 (released July 2024) and Mistral’s Mixtral 8x22B (released April 2024) are distributed as weight files without the training data transparency that the EU AI Act now mandates for proprietary API providers. This creates an asymmetry: a buyer deploying an open-weight model cannot rely on provider indemnification and cannot easily verify the legality of the training corpus. Meta’s acceptable use policy for Llama 3.1 prohibits use in litigation against Meta, a clause that some legal commentators have flagged as potentially limiting a licensee’s ability to seek contribution if the licensee is sued for copyright infringement based on the model’s outputs.

The countervailing advantage is that open-weight models allow buyers to implement their own compliance controls, including output filtering, prompt guardrails, and retrieval-augmented generation architectures that ground outputs in licensed data sources. For organizations with mature legal and compliance functions, this control may outweigh the lack of indemnification. For smaller teams without dedicated IP counsel, proprietary models with contractual protections remain the safer default.

## Industry-Specific Exposure Analysis

### Enterprise SaaS and Customer-Facing Applications

Organizations deploying AI features in customer-facing products face the highest exposure to training data copyright claims. A SaaS provider using an unlicensed model to generate marketing copy, product descriptions, or customer communications risks direct infringement claims if the model reproduces copyrighted material. The risk compounds in industries with well-organized rights holder groups. Getty Images filed its landmark complaint against Stability AI in February 2023 in the U.S. District Court for the District of Delaware, alleging that Stability AI copied more than 12 million images from Getty’s database without permission. The case survived a motion to dismiss in October 2023 and is scheduled for trial in September 2025. The outcome will directly affect any company using image generation models trained on unlicensed visual content.

For text-based applications, the practical mitigation strategy adopted by most enterprises is retrieval-augmented generation with a curated, licensed knowledge base. By grounding model outputs in a controlled corpus where the enterprise holds or has licensed the underlying rights, the risk of reproducing infringing content drops substantially. This approach does not eliminate the risk that the underlying model itself was trained on infringing data, but it creates a strong factual record that the enterprise took reasonable steps to prevent infringement—a factor that courts weigh in assessing willfulness and damages.

### Internal Enterprise Deployments

Internal use cases—code generation for proprietary software, document summarization for internal reports, data analysis on company-owned datasets—carry lower but non-zero copyright risk. The primary exposure arises when internal tools generate outputs that are later incorporated into external-facing products or communications. A developer using GitHub Copilot to generate a function that reproduces GPL-licensed code, then shipping that code in a proprietary product, creates a copyright compliance issue that may not surface until a software audit or acquisition due diligence process.

Microsoft’s indemnification for GitHub Copilot, announced September 2023, covers customers who use the tool with its optional duplication detection filter enabled. The filter checks generated code against public repositories and flags matches. As of March 2025, Microsoft reports that approximately 1.2% of Copilot suggestions trigger the duplication filter, a figure that has remained stable since the filter’s introduction. For enterprise buyers, requiring developers to enable this filter is a low-cost risk mitigation measure that preserves indemnification coverage.

### Regulated Industries

Financial services, healthcare, and legal technology sectors face additional compliance obligations that intersect with training data copyright issues. The EU’s Digital Operational Resilience Act (DORA), effective January 17, 2025, requires financial institutions to maintain full audit trails of third-party technology providers, including AI model vendors. A bank using an AI model for credit risk assessment must document the model’s training data provenance as part of its third-party risk management framework. The absence of a transparent data lineage from a model provider can create a DORA compliance gap that regulators may treat as a deficiency in operational resilience.

In healthcare, the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services issued guidance on December 12, 2024, clarifying that AI models used in clinical decision support must comply with HIPAA’s minimum necessary standard. While this guidance primarily addresses patient data privacy, it signals a regulatory expectation that covered entities understand the data flows underlying their AI tools—including training data. A hospital using an AI transcription service trained on unlicensed copyrighted medical textbooks faces a compound risk: potential copyright liability and a regulatory finding that the hospital failed to exercise appropriate vendor due diligence.

## Actionable Recommendations

First, require a written training data disclosure from every model provider before signing an enterprise agreement. This disclosure should specify the categories of data sources, the legal basis claimed for copyrighted material, and the provider’s indemnification scope and monetary cap. If a provider cannot or will not provide this disclosure, treat the model as carrying elevated legal risk and price that risk into the deployment decision. A model that costs $0.03 per 1,000 tokens but exposes the organization to uncapped copyright liability may be more expensive than a model at $0.12 per 1,000 tokens with full indemnification.

Second, implement output filtering and monitoring as a technical safeguard regardless of the model’s training data status. Copyright infringement claims typically require proof that the defendant reproduced protected expression. A robust output filter that detects and blocks potentially infringing content before it reaches end users creates a factual record of reasonable prevention efforts. Several commercial tools now offer copyright detection for AI outputs, including Copyleaks (launched AI Content Detector with copyright flagging in January 2024) and Originality.ai (added training data similarity scoring in Q3 2024).

Third, segment model usage by risk tier and apply different procurement standards accordingly. For internal, non-customer-facing applications with low output volume, the risk of a copyright claim is minimal and the cost of full indemnification may not be justified. For customer-facing applications generating public-facing content at scale, full indemnification from a Tier 1 provider is the only defensible position. Document this risk-tiering decision internally to establish a record of reasonable risk management.

Fourth, monitor the Supreme Court’s certiorari docket for the 2025-2026 term. If the Court grants review in *New York Times v. Microsoft* or *Kadrey v. Meta*, the resulting decision will establish nationwide precedent on training data fair use. A ruling that training data ingestion is not fair use would fundamentally alter the economics of foundation model development and likely trigger a wave of licensing agreements that raise API prices industry-wide. Procurement teams should model this scenario and lock in multi-year pricing with indemnification before any adverse ruling shifts the market.

Fifth, for organizations deploying open-weight models, invest in data provenance tooling and legal review of fine-tuning datasets. The cost of a copyright infringement defense—even a successful one—will exceed the cost of proactive data governance. A single motion to dismiss in a copyright case typically costs $50,000 to $150,000 in legal fees. Spending $10,000 to $25,000 on data provenance tooling and an initial legal review of training data is a rational investment that reduces the probability of facing that defense cost in the first place.
