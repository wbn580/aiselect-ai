The decision to adopt a formal experiment tracking system in mid-2024 is no longer a question of engineering maturity; it is a direct response to the economics of GPU compute and the fragmentation of the model landscape. As of October 2024, the cost of renting an 8×H100 node on spot markets fluctuates wildly between $2.50 and $4.80 per GPU-hour, making a single 72-hour full fine-tune of Llama-3.1-70B a $4,320–$8,294 gamble before factoring in the fully burdened cost of a senior ML engineer. Simultaneously, the shift from monolithic model providers to compound AI systems—where a single inference call might chain gpt-4o-2024-08-06, a self-hosted vLLM instance, and a Pinecone vector lookup—has blown out the surface area for regressions. A prompt tweak that improves accuracy on a held-out eval set by 2.3 percentage points can silently degrade retrieval recall by 4.1 points, a delta that remains invisible without lineage tracking. The MLOps tooling market, which consolidated rapidly through 2022 and 2023, now presents a clear bifurcation: platforms that treat experiment tracking as a standalone utility versus those that embed it as the spine of an opinionated workflow. For teams provisioning their 2025 infrastructure budgets in Q4 2024, the choice among Weights & Biases (W&B), MLflow, and Neptune locks in not just a logging surface but a specific posture toward reproducibility, collaboration, and cost attribution.

## The Core Tracking Surface: What Gets Logged and How

The minimum viable experiment tracker must capture three artifacts: hyperparameters, metrics, and a pointer to the data snapshot. The differentiation among the three platforms lies in how aggressively they enforce schema, how they handle large media artifacts, and the granularity of their system metric collection.

### Schema Enforcement and Nested Logging

W&B operates on an implicit, dictionary-based schema where `wandb.log()` accepts arbitrary key-value pairs at each step. This flexibility is a known footgun: a typo in a metric name (`val_loss` vs `val_loss_v2`) creates a silent fork in the run history that downstream comparison queries will treat as distinct series. W&B’s 0.18.5 SDK (September 2024) introduced optional schema validation through `wandb.define_metric()`, but adoption remains opt-in. MLflow 2.14.1, by contrast, enforces no schema at all at the logging layer; its tracking server accepts arbitrary params, metrics, and tags as flat dictionaries, deferring all structure to the query layer. Neptune’s 1.10.0 client takes the strictest approach, requiring users to declare a run’s structure via the `namespace` abstraction—a hierarchical dictionary where `model/encoder/layers` and `model/decoder/layers` are distinct leaves that the UI renders as a collapsible tree. This rigidity pays off in multi-team settings: a Neptune project with 1,200 runs across four teams at a European fintech (as reported in their Q3 2024 case study) showed zero metric name collisions over six months, a problem that had plagued their prior MLflow instance.

### Artifact Handling and Large Media

The treatment of artifacts—model checkpoints, dataset hashes, segmentation masks—separates the platforms on storage economics. W&B’s artifact system versions blobs with content-addressable references and deduplicates across runs within a project. A 7.4 GB LoRA adapter logged by two separate runs that share the same base model will consume 7.4 GB of W&B’s managed storage, not 14.8 GB. MLflow’s artifact store is a pass-through to the configured backend (S3, GCS, or NFS); it performs no deduplication and relies on the user to implement content hashing. Neptune stores artifacts in its managed object store and applies deduplication at the project level, but its artifact browser caps individual file previews at 100 MB as of the 2024.3 platform release, making it unsuitable for teams that need to visually inspect full-resolution diffusion model outputs without downloading. For a computer vision team generating 512×512 RGB outputs at 10 frames per second during a 6-hour training run, the artifact volume reaches approximately 41.5 GB per run. On W&B’s Team plan at $8 per user per month plus $0.001 per GB-month for overage storage, that run’s artifacts cost $0.0415 per month to retain; on a self-hosted MLflow instance backed by S3, the storage cost is $0.023 per GB-month (us-east-1 standard tier as of October 2024), or $0.9545 per month, but the engineering overhead of maintaining the MLflow tracking server and S3 lifecycle policies is not zero.

## Collaboration Models and the Lineage Problem

Experiment tracking is a team sport, but the three platforms have diverged on what constitutes a collaborative primitive. The question is no longer whether a platform supports sharing a URL to a run dashboard—all three do—but whether the platform can reconstruct the exact state that produced a given model artifact six months after the fact.

### Run Comparison and Report Generation

W&B’s comparison interface operates on a workspace model: a drag-and-drop grid of panels where each panel is a query against the run history (parallel coordinate plots, parameter importance charts, custom Vega-Lite specs). Workspaces are shareable via URL, but the underlying queries are mutable; a workspace saved in October 2024 that filters for `dataset_version == “2024-09-15”` will silently include new runs if the project continues logging runs with that tag. MLflow’s compare UI is read-only and stateless: selecting runs from the experiment list generates a temporary comparison view with no persistence beyond the URL. Neptune’s dashboard system is the most opinionated, treating dashboards as versioned JSON artifacts that can be committed to a Git repository alongside code. A Neptune dashboard saved as `neptune_dashboards/regression_suite.json` and checked into the project repo at commit `a3f2b1c` is reproducible by design, though the platform’s UI for editing these dashboards remains less fluid than W&B’s drag-and-drop canvas as of the 2024.3 release.

### Data and Code Lineage

The deeper problem is lineage: given a model checkpoint that achieved 0.891 F1 on a production eval set, can the platform answer which exact dataset slice, code commit, and environment produced it? W&B’s artifact DAG (directed acyclic graph) constructs this lineage automatically when users log artifacts with `used_by` and `produces` relationships, but the graph is only as complete as the logging discipline of the team. A W&B project at a U.S. autonomous vehicle company, described in their 2024 ICML workshop paper, achieved full lineage coverage for 94% of production models by enforcing artifact logging through a custom training harness that wraps PyTorch Lightning. MLflow’s model registry provides lineage at the model version level—each version links to the run that produced it—but does not automatically capture data lineage unless the user explicitly logs the dataset URI as a tag. Neptune’s approach is to treat lineage as a first-class object: the `neptune.fetch_runs_table()` API returns a pandas DataFrame where each run’s code commit, data checksum, and environment hash are queryable columns, provided they were logged. The gap between “provided they were logged” and “guaranteed” is the same across all three platforms; none can retroactively infer lineage from an unlogged run.

## Pricing and Infrastructure Lock-in

The pricing models as of October 2024 reflect each vendor’s bet on where value accrues in the MLOps stack. W&B charges per user per month with tiered feature gates; MLflow is open-source with optional Databricks managed hosting; Neptune charges per tracked hour with a free tier capped at 100 hours per month.

### W&B Pricing and the GPU Tax

W&B’s pricing as of October 2024 stands at $8 per user per month for the Team plan (up to 50 users, 100 GB storage pooled) and $25 per user per month for the Enterprise plan (unlimited users, SSO, audit logs, and a dedicated artifact registry). The critical detail is that W&B does not charge for compute—a team of 5 engineers running 200 experiments per month on an 8×A100 cluster pays $40 per month in platform fees, not a function of GPU-hours consumed. This pricing model aligns W&B’s incentives with high-velocity teams that run many experiments, but it creates a subtle pressure to log everything, which in turn increases storage costs. A team logging system metrics at 1 Hz across 8 GPUs for a 72-hour run generates approximately 2,073,600 data points per run. W&B’s backend samples these to 1,500 points per metric for display but stores the full resolution; the storage cost for metadata is negligible compared to artifact storage, but the query performance on projects with 10,000+ runs degrades noticeably, a pain point acknowledged in W&B’s September 2024 status page incident report that detailed a 47-minute query timeout on a project with 34,000 runs.

### MLflow’s Zero License Cost and Hidden Ops Burden

MLflow’s tracking server is Apache 2.0 licensed with no usage-based fees. The cost is the engineering time to deploy, monitor, and migrate the backing database and artifact store. A production MLflow deployment on AWS with a PostgreSQL RDS instance (db.t4g.medium, $0.036 per hour), an S3 artifact bucket, and an EC2 instance (m7i.large, $0.096 per hour) running the tracking server costs approximately $94.61 per month in infrastructure before labor. Databricks’ managed MLflow, included with Databricks workspaces, removes this operational burden but ties the tracking surface to the Databricks ecosystem. A team using Databricks’ MLflow on AWS pays the underlying infrastructure costs plus Databricks’ DBU surcharge, which for a standard all-purpose compute cluster is $0.40 per DBU-hour. A single 72-hour training run on a cluster consuming 8 DBU-hours per hour costs $230.40 in Databricks platform fees, dwarfing the $40 per month W&B charges for the entire team. The calculus flips for teams that are already on Databricks for Spark workloads; the marginal cost of using MLflow is zero.

### Neptune’s Hourly Metering

Neptune’s pricing as of October 2024 is $29 per user per month for the Team plan, which includes 500 tracked hours per month, with overage at $0.05 per tracked hour. A tracked hour is defined as any hour in which a run is actively logging data; paused or completed runs do not count. For a team of 5 running 200 experiments per month with an average duration of 3 hours, the total tracked hours are 600, yielding a bill of $29 × 5 + $100 × $0.05 = $145 + $5 = $150 per month. This model penalizes long-running experiments—a single 720-hour (30-day) RL training run consumes 720 tracked hours, costing $36 in overage alone. Neptune’s free tier (100 tracked hours per month, 1 user, 20 GB storage) is the most generous entry point for solo practitioners, but the cliff to the Team plan is steep for small teams that exceed the hourly cap.

## Closing Takeaways

The experiment tracking decision in Q4 2024 hinges on three variables: team size, experiment velocity, and the degree of infrastructure control the team is willing to sacrifice.

First, teams of 1–3 engineers who run fewer than 100 tracked hours per month and value zero-config setup should start with Neptune’s free tier. The namespace-based schema enforcement prevents the metric hygiene problems that plague solo projects as they scale, and the hourly metering cap is effectively irrelevant at this volume.

Second, teams already on Databricks for data engineering should use MLflow and invest the savings in a dedicated MLOps engineer who can enforce logging conventions at the training harness level. The platform fee arbitrage—$230.40 per long-running experiment on Databricks versus $40 per month for W&B—only makes sense if the team leverages MLflow’s zero marginal cost within the Databricks ecosystem. Outside Databricks, the operational burden of self-hosting MLflow consumes the savings for teams smaller than 10 engineers.

Third, teams running 200+ experiments per month with artifact volumes exceeding 50 GB per month should negotiate an Enterprise deal with W&B. The artifact deduplication alone can reduce storage costs by 40–60% for teams that frequently iterate on fine-tunes of the same base model, and the artifact DAG lineage becomes a hard requirement when regulatory audit trails are necessary.

Fourth, no platform solves the garbage-in, garbage-out problem. A team that does not enforce a logging standard—metric naming conventions, mandatory dataset checksums, environment hashes—will produce an unqueryable morass of runs on any platform. The first $10,000 of MLOps investment should go to a training harness that bakes these conventions into the experiment launch path, not to the tracking platform itself.
