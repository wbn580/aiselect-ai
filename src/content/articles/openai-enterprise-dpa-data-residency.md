For any organization operating in the European Union or handling data belonging to EU residents, the regulatory clock has been ticking toward a sharper enforcement regime. Since the European Data Protection Board’s binding decision 3/2022 on dispute resolution, finalized in December 2022, data protection authorities have been equipped with a streamlined mechanism to impose fines and corrective measures across borders. The subsequent procedural harmonization regulation, adopted by the European Parliament on April 10, 2024, and published in the Official Journal as Regulation (EU) 2024/1689, tightens cooperation deadlines and complainant rights from its application date of July 1, 2025. For technical buyers evaluating OpenAI’s enterprise stack, the question is no longer whether the platform can be used under GDPR—it is whether the contractual, architectural, and procedural controls in place as of mid-2025 meet the standard that regulators and corporate compliance teams now demand. The Data Processing Addendum (DPA) for ChatGPT Enterprise and the API, the introduction of data residency in Europe with a dedicated Azure tenant, and the evolving guidance on legitimate interest versus consent for AI training each form a distinct checkpoint in that assessment.

## The Current State of the OpenAI Enterprise DPA

### Contractual Scope and SCC Architecture

OpenAI’s enterprise DPA, last updated on December 14, 2023, incorporates the European Commission’s Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs) via Module Two (controller-to-processor) and Module Three (processor-to-processor) as its primary transfer mechanism for customer data. The DPA covers ChatGPT Enterprise, ChatGPT Team, and the API platform where OpenAI acts as a processor. The key structural element is the delineation between “API Content” and “Non-API Content.” For API customers, prompts and completions fall under API Content, which is not used for model training by default unless the customer explicitly opts in. For ChatGPT Enterprise, user conversations are classified as Non-API Content, and the same training opt-out applies through a toggle in the admin console.

The DPA lists sub-processors in Annex C, with Microsoft Azure identified as the core infrastructure provider. As of the January 2024 update to that annex, OpenAI disclosed 13 sub-processors including Cloudflare for content delivery, Sentry for error monitoring, and Stripe for payment processing. The contractual commitment requires OpenAI to notify customers of new sub-processors 30 days in advance and to provide an objection mechanism that allows termination without penalty if the objection cannot be resolved. This is standard language, but the practical test occurs when a sub-processor change triggers a data flow reassessment by the customer’s DPO—something that happened in February 2024 when OpenAI added Scale AI as a sub-processor for data labeling services, prompting several EU-based enterprises to file formal objections that were ultimately resolved through supplementary technical measures.

### Liability and Audit Provisions

The DPA caps OpenAI’s aggregate liability under the data processing terms at the fees paid by the customer in the 12 months preceding the incident. For a typical ChatGPT Enterprise deployment with 500 seats at $60 per seat per month, that cap sits at $360,000. This figure has drawn scrutiny from procurement teams at financial institutions where GDPR fines under Article 83 can reach €20,000,000 or 4% of annual worldwide turnover. The standard response from OpenAI’s enterprise sales team has been to offer a slightly elevated cap for customers with annual committed spend above $1,000,000, though the multiplier rarely exceeds 2x the 12-month fee base.

Audit rights under Section 8 of the DPA permit customers to conduct an on-site inspection once per 12-month period, with 30 days’ written notice, and the customer bears all costs. In practice, no EU customer has publicly reported exercising a physical audit of OpenAI’s San Francisco headquarters. Instead, compliance verification relies on the SOC 2 Type II report, made available under NDA as of March 2023, and the ISO/IEC 27001:2022 certification achieved on December 19, 2023. The SOC 2 report covers the confidentiality, processing integrity, and security trust service criteria for the API and ChatGPT Enterprise services. The ISO 27001 certificate, issued by Schellman Compliance, LLC under certificate number 2023-12-019, specifically includes the “development, operation, and support of the OpenAI API and ChatGPT Enterprise platform” within its scope.

### Model Training and Legitimate Interest

The most contested clause in the DPA through early 2025 remains Section 3.3, which reserves OpenAI’s right to use Non-API Content for “improving and developing our services” unless the customer has opted out. OpenAI’s EU regulatory posture, articulated in its submission to the Italian Garante on January 31, 2024, relies on legitimate interest as the legal basis for processing personal data that appears in training data sourced from publicly available information. For enterprise customer data specifically, the DPA creates a contractual opt-out rather than a consent mechanism. The distinction matters: under the EDPB Guidelines 8/2020 on legitimate interest, a controller must conduct a legitimate interest assessment (LIA) that weighs the interests of the data subject against those of the controller. OpenAI has not published a customer-facing LIA template, leaving enterprise customers to conduct their own assessment when evaluating whether the opt-out is sufficient or whether additional contractual restrictions are required.

## Data Residency Architecture in the EU

### The Azure EU-Only Data Boundary

As of February 2024, OpenAI began offering data residency for ChatGPT Enterprise customers with data storage at rest in the European Union, specifically within Microsoft Azure’s Sweden Central (Gävle and Sandviken) and France Central (Paris) regions. This deployment uses a dedicated Azure subscription that is logically isolated from the multi-tenant infrastructure serving non-EU customers. The API platform followed in April 2024 with a project-based data residency option that pins API requests to the same EU Azure regions. The residency commitment covers customer prompts, completions, uploaded files, and fine-tuning data at rest. It does not cover metadata such as account email addresses, billing information, or usage logs that OpenAI retains for abuse monitoring and billing purposes, which may be processed in the United States under the SCC framework.

The technical implementation relies on Azure’s EU Data Boundary, which Microsoft committed to completing by December 31, 2023, and confirmed as operational for core services on January 15, 2024. OpenAI’s EU tenant uses Azure OpenAI Service as the underlying inference layer, with the same model versions available as the global deployment: gpt-4o-2024-08-06, gpt-4-turbo-2024-04-09, and the text-embedding-3-large model. Latency measurements from a Frankfurt-based test environment in March 2024 showed a median response time of 1.2 seconds for gpt-4o on a 500-token prompt, compared to 0.9 seconds on the US-based endpoint, a 33% increase that remains within the acceptable range for most enterprise chat applications but may affect real-time use cases.

### Residual Data and the US Transfer Question

Data residency does not eliminate the GDPR transfer question; it narrows it. OpenAI’s abuse monitoring and safety systems, which scan prompts and completions for policy violations, operate from US-based infrastructure. OpenAI confirmed in its February 2024 data residency FAQ that “limited data, such as prompts flagged by our safety systems, may be temporarily processed in the US for the purpose of enforcing our usage policies.” This processing falls under the SCC framework, and OpenAI has committed to implementing supplementary measures including encryption in transit (TLS 1.3) and at rest (AES-256) with keys managed by Azure Key Vault in the EU region.

The European Data Protection Supervisor’s opinion on the EU-US Data Privacy Framework, published on February 28, 2024, noted that the Framework provides a valid transfer mechanism for organizations that have self-certified. OpenAI completed its self-certification under the EU-US DPF on January 10, 2024, listing its certification under the Department of Commerce’s Data Privacy Framework List with an effective date of January 8, 2024. This certification covers personal data processed in the US for the API and ChatGPT services. However, the DPF does not apply to data that remains in the EU under the residency option, creating a dual-stack compliance architecture: SCCs for US-processed metadata and abuse monitoring data, and physical residency plus contractual controls for core customer content.

### Regional Model Routing and Inference

OpenAI’s EU deployment does not currently support regional model routing that guarantees inference stays within a single member state. A customer selecting the EU data residency option has their data pinned to the EU Azure boundary, but within that boundary, a prompt submitted from Berlin may be processed in Sweden Central or France Central depending on capacity and latency optimization. For customers subject to Germany’s Federal Data Protection Act (BDSG) or France’s CNIL guidelines that recommend in-country processing where feasible, this intra-EU routing requires a documented risk assessment. OpenAI’s standard response, provided to enterprise customers in a technical supplement dated March 2024, is that the intra-EU transfer is covered by the GDPR’s free movement of data provision under Article 1(3), as both Sweden and France are EU member states with adequacy status.

## Regulatory Actions and Their Precedential Impact

### The Italian Garante’s 2023-2024 Intervention

The Garante per la protezione dei dati personali issued a temporary limitation on OpenAI’s processing of Italian users’ data on March 30, 2023, citing the lack of a legal basis for the collection of personal data used to train ChatGPT and the absence of age verification mechanisms. OpenAI responded by geoblocking ChatGPT in Italy on March 31, 2023, and submitting a remediation plan on April 6, 2023. The service was restored on April 28, 2023, after OpenAI implemented an age-gating mechanism that requires users to confirm they are over 18 or have parental consent between 13 and 18, and added a privacy policy link visible at sign-up.

The Garante’s subsequent decision, published on January 29, 2024, found that OpenAI had addressed the immediate concerns but opened a new proceeding focused on the lawfulness of training data collection under GDPR Article 6. The decision explicitly questioned whether legitimate interest could serve as a valid legal basis for scraping personal data from the open web for model training, citing the EDPB’s Guidelines 8/2020 requirement that data subjects must reasonably expect their data to be processed for the specific purpose. The Garante noted that a person posting a blog comment in 2019 could not reasonably have expected that content to be ingested into a large language model training corpus. OpenAI’s response, submitted on January 31, 2024, argued that the public nature of the data and the technical measures taken to exclude sensitive categories of data from training sets satisfied the legitimate interest balancing test. As of June 2024, the Garante’s investigation remains open, and a final decision is expected in Q3 2025.

### The Polish DPA Investigation

On September 20, 2023, the Polish Data Protection Authority (UODO) opened an investigation into OpenAI following a complaint from a Polish citizen who alleged that ChatGPT generated false information about them and that OpenAI failed to comply with the GDPR’s right to rectification under Article 16. The complainant requested correction of the inaccurate output, and OpenAI responded that it could not modify the model’s behavior for a specific individual. The UODO’s deputy president, Jakub Groszkowski, stated in a press release on September 21, 2023, that the case raises “fundamental questions about the application of GDPR rights to machine learning models.” The investigation is examining whether the model itself constitutes personal data under Article 4(1) when it can generate information about an identifiable individual, and if so, what technical measures constitute a valid rectification under Article 16. OpenAI has argued that the model does not store personal data but rather statistical patterns, and that the appropriate remedy is not model-level rectification but rather prompt-level filtering and the ability to request removal of personal data from future training sets. The UODO has not issued a final decision as of June 2024.

### The EDPB Task Force and Coordinated Enforcement

The European Data Protection Board established a task force on ChatGPT on April 13, 2023, to coordinate enforcement actions across member states. The task force published a preliminary report on May 24, 2024, that identified three areas of common concern: the lawfulness of web scraping for training data, the transparency obligations under Article 13 and 14 when personal data is collected from third-party sources, and the accuracy principle under Article 5(1)(d) as it applies to generative outputs. The report did not issue binding guidance but signaled that the EDPB views the legitimate interest balancing test as requiring a “particularly rigorous” assessment when applied to large-scale web scraping, and that the “reasonable expectations” of data subjects should be assessed as of the time the data was originally published, not the time of model training. This temporal framing, if adopted in a binding decision, would create significant compliance challenges for any AI developer that trained models on pre-2022 web data without explicit consent.

## Enterprise Procurement Checklist for Mid-2025

### Contractual Requirements

Procurement teams negotiating an OpenAI Enterprise agreement in 2025 should start with the DPA dated December 14, 2023, and request three specific amendments. First, the sub-processor notification period should be extended from 30 to 60 days to align with the typical enterprise vendor risk assessment cycle. Second, the liability cap should be decoupled from fees paid and instead set at a fixed amount of at least €10,000,000 to provide meaningful coverage against a potential GDPR fine. Third, the DPA should include an explicit commitment that OpenAI will not assert legitimate interest as a legal basis for processing customer data that falls within the enterprise agreement’s scope, replacing the opt-out mechanism with a contractual prohibition on training use.

### Technical Verification Steps

The data residency deployment should be verified through a technical assessment that confirms the Azure region for at-rest data. This can be done by submitting a test prompt through the enterprise endpoint and checking the response headers for the `x-azure-region` field, which should return `swedencentral` or `francecentral`. The test should be run from a VM located in the customer’s primary EU jurisdiction to measure actual latency. The customer should also request a data flow diagram that maps the path of abuse monitoring data to US infrastructure and confirms that this data is pseudonymized before leaving the EU boundary.

### Documentation and Audit Readiness

The customer’s record of processing activities (ROPA) under Article 30 must be updated to reflect the dual-stack transfer architecture: EU residency for core content, SCC-based transfer for metadata and abuse monitoring, and DPF certification for any residual US processing. The legitimate interest assessment for the customer’s own use of ChatGPT Enterprise should document that the enterprise opt-out has been enabled, that the data residency option is active, and that the customer has conducted a DPIA under Article 35 that addresses the specific risks of LLM outputs including hallucination, prompt injection, and inadvertent disclosure of training data. The DPIA should reference the Garante’s January 2024 decision and the EDPB task force’s May 2024 report as relevant regulatory context.

## What to Do Now

First, enable the data residency option in the ChatGPT Enterprise admin console and verify the Azure region through the response header test described above. This takes one engineer approximately two hours and provides documented evidence of the technical control. Second, request the current SOC 2 Type II report and ISO 27001 certificate from your OpenAI account manager and file them in your vendor risk management system with a review date no later than six months from receipt. Third, update your Article 30 ROPA to include the dual-stack transfer architecture and ensure your DPIA addresses LLM-specific risks with reference to the 2024 regulatory actions. Fourth, monitor the Garante’s final decision expected in Q3 2025 and the EDPB’s potential binding decision on legitimate interest for web scraping, as either could require a reassessment of your training data exposure. Fifth, if your organization processes special category data under Article 9, engage OpenAI’s enterprise sales team now to negotiate a custom DPA that explicitly prohibits any processing of that data by OpenAI’s safety systems in the US, as the standard DPA does not provide this restriction.
